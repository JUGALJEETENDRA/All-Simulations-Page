Briefing Red Bull Stratos
Digital Business Transformation Report

Red Bull Stratos

Integrating Owned, Earned and Paid Media to Create a Global Media Event

This case study analyzes the integration of owned, earned, and paid digital media, using Red Bull Stratos as a model.[1] It details how Red Bull moved beyond conventional product advertising to establish itself as a direct content creator and publisher.[3, 5] By leveraging high-altitude freefall telemetry and live YouTube streaming, the study demonstrates how owned content creates a self-sustaining media amplification loop, transforming brand communication in a digital business environment.[2, 4]

Section 1: Digital Media Types & The Stratos Launch #

The growth of digital media has transformed the way businesses communicate with consumers. Traditional marketing relied heavily on organizations purchasing advertising space in television, newspapers, radio and outdoor media. In the digital environment, however, brands can communicate through a combination of owned, earned and paid media channels.

These media types are defined as follows:[3]

  • Owned Media: Communication channels and content controlled by an organization, such as its website, branded content platforms and official social-media channels.
  • Paid Media: Paying for exposure through advertising, sponsorship, promoted content and media placements.
  • Earned Media: The visibility gained when consumers, journalists and other external parties voluntarily discuss, share or report on a brand.

A powerful industrial example of the interaction between these three media types is Red Bull Stratos, a high-altitude freefall project created by Red Bull.[1] On 14 October 2012, Austrian skydiver Felix Baumgartner ascended into the stratosphere in a helium-balloon-supported capsule before performing a historic freefall from approximately 38,969 metres above Earth. The mission became a global media phenomenon as audiences around the world followed the event through digital platforms, television, news coverage and social discussion.[2]

Red Bull Stratos was therefore more than a conventional advertising campaign. Rather than simply purchasing media attention, Red Bull created an event that itself became globally valuable media content. The central lesson of the case is:

Red Bull used owned media to create and distribute the story, generated massive earned media by making the story newsworthy, and used the wider promotional ecosystem to amplify its reach.

Section 2: The Marketing Challenge: Competing for Attention #

The modern media environment presents a fundamental problem for brands: consumers are exposed to enormous amounts of advertising and can increasingly ignore, skip or avoid conventional promotional messages. For an energy-drink company such as Red Bull, conventional advertising could communicate product features or brand messages, but it would be difficult for ordinary advertisements alone to create a globally distinctive brand identity. The strategic challenge was:

How could Red Bull gain global consumer attention without depending entirely on traditional product advertising?

Red Bull developed a broader approach centred on sports, adventure, culture and branded experiences. Instead of communicating only “Buy this energy drink,” the brand increasingly created content and experiences associated with: **Adventure + performance + risk + achievement + pushing human limits**.[1]

Red Bull Stratos represented an extreme expression of this strategy. The project was designed around a genuine technical and human challenge that could attract audiences independently of the beverage itself. This changed the conventional relationship between a brand and media:

  • Traditional advertising model: Brand → Pays media company → Advertisement → Audience.
  • Red Bull Stratos model: Brand → Creates extraordinary event → Produces content → Audience watches → Media reports → Public shares and discusses.

The campaign therefore provides an excellent case for understanding how owned, earned and paid media can interact rather than operate as isolated channels.[3]

The Red Bull Stratos Project

Red Bull Stratos was a high-altitude balloon and freefall project involving a multidisciplinary team and skydiver Felix Baumgartner. On 14 October 2012, Baumgartner reached an altitude of 38,969 metres before jumping from the capsule and descending toward Earth. The mission attracted attention because it combined several powerful elements: an extreme human challenge, advanced technology and engineering, scientific and medical objectives, uncertainty and risk, a live global event, and a strong connection with Red Bull's established brand identity.[2]

The event was therefore naturally suited to digital media. Unlike a conventional advertisement with a predetermined short duration, Stratos created a continuing narrative: **Mission announcement → Technical preparation → Testing and training → Anticipation and media coverage → Live launch and ascent → Historic jump → Post-event videos, reporting and discussion**.

The project effectively became a long-form brand story rather than a single advertising message.

Section 3: Branded Brackets: Owned, Earned and Paid Media #

Owned Media: Red Bull Became the Content Creator

The first component of the media strategy was owned media. Owned media consists of communication assets over which a company has substantial control. In the Red Bull Stratos case, the brand was not merely the advertiser attached to somebody else's event. Red Bull was central to the project and its content ecosystem. The campaign generated a wide range of branded material around: mission preparation, technical development, test jumps, team members, behind-the-scenes content, photographs, videos, live coverage, and post-event documentaries and highlights.[1]

The most significant digital distribution point was Red Bull's presence on YouTube, where the mission was livestreamed to a global audience. YouTube reported that more than 8 million concurrent livestreams were watching at the peak of the event.[4] This demonstrated the strategic power of owned media:

Red Bull did not have to compress the entire brand story into a 30-second advertisement. It could create an extensive content ecosystem that audiences actively chose to watch.

The audience's relationship with the content was therefore fundamentally different from conventional advertising:

  • Conventional paid advertisement: Audience wants entertainment → Advertisement interrupts.
  • Red Bull Stratos content: Audience actively chooses to watch → Brand is embedded within the experience.

This represents an important evolution in digital media strategy. The company itself can behave as a content producer and publisher, reducing its dependence on traditional media organizations for direct audience communication.

Earned Media: Turning a Brand Project into Global News

The second and arguably most powerful component was earned media. Earned media occurs when external parties voluntarily provide attention to a company or campaign. This may include: news coverage, television reporting, online articles, social-media discussion, audience sharing, commentary, and word-of-mouth. Red Bull Stratos possessed a major advantage: it was genuinely newsworthy.

Journalists were not merely being asked to report on a new energy-drink advertisement. They were covering a dramatic high-altitude mission involving human performance, engineering, risk and record-breaking achievement. The event therefore crossed multiple media categories: Sports media (extreme performance), Science and technology media (high-altitude equipment), Mainstream news (historic global event), Social media (live sharing and discussion), and Entertainment media (spectacle and suspense). This generated a powerful earned-media effect: **Red Bull creates the event → Journalists consider it newsworthy → News organizations report it → Audiences discuss and share it → Additional people discover the event → Further media attention is generated**.

This is fundamentally different from paid advertising because Red Bull did not need to purchase every individual article, conversation or social share. The event demonstrates: The strongest earned media is often generated when a brand creates something that people would want to discuss even if it were not an advertisement.

Paid Media: Amplifying the Wider Campaign

Paid media represents exposure obtained through paid promotional activity, including advertising, sponsorship-related promotion, promoted content and other purchased media opportunities. In the Red Bull Stratos case, paid media should be understood as the amplification layer, rather than the sole foundation of the campaign. This distinction is important.

A conventional campaign may begin with an advertisement and then purchase media space to distribute it. Stratos began with a much larger content asset: the mission itself. Paid promotional activity could then support awareness and direct audiences towards the wider content ecosystem. The strategic relationship can be represented as: **Paid media** (creates or extends awareness) → **Owned media** (provides destination and deeper experience) → **Earned media** (multiplies reach through journalism and public discussion).

Therefore, paid media did not operate independently. Its value was strengthened because audiences who discovered the project could find extensive owned content, while widespread earned attention further amplified the campaign. A useful academic interpretation is:

"Paid media can buy initial or additional reach, but owned and earned media can extend the depth, duration and credibility of audience engagement."

For this reason, Red Bull Stratos should not be presented as a campaign where the three media categories were necessarily equal in scale. Its exceptional strength came particularly from the combination of a powerful owned-content platform and enormous earned attention, supported by a broader promotional ecosystem.

Section 4: Media Integration & Amplification Loops #

The true strength of the Red Bull Stratos case was not the use of any single media category. It was the interaction among them.

Integration of Owned, Earned and Paid Media

Media Type Role in Red Bull Stratos Strategic Value
Owned Media Branded mission content, videos, digital channels and livestream presence Gave Red Bull control over storytelling and long-form content
Earned Media News reporting, public discussion, social sharing and external commentary Expanded reach and added third-party attention
Paid Media Promotional amplification and wider campaign support Extended awareness and directed attention towards the campaign
Integrated Effect Each media category strengthened the others Turned a branded project into a global media event

The interaction can be represented as: **PAID MEDIA** (Generates and amplifies awareness) → **OWNED MEDIA** (Provides content and brand-controlled experience) → **EARNED MEDIA** (Audiences and external media amplify) → **MORE ATTENTION FLOWS BACK TO OWNED MEDIA**. This creates a **media amplification loop**. For example: A person discovers the mission through media coverage → Visits the livestream or branded content → Watches the event → Shares or discusses it → Creates additional earned exposure → More people discover the owned content. The audience therefore becomes part of the communication process.

From Advertising to Brand-Owned Media

One of the most important strategic lessons from Red Bull Stratos is the changing role of the company itself. In the traditional model: Companies create products. Media companies create content. Companies pay media companies for audience access. In the Red Bull model: The company creates the product, creates the event, produces the content and distributes that content directly to audiences. Red Bull therefore operated not only as an energy-drink company but also as a media creator.

This does not mean traditional advertising became irrelevant. Rather, the boundaries between brand, publisher, content producer, event organizer, and media distributor became increasingly blurred. This is particularly important in digital business because organizations now have direct access to audiences through websites, video platforms and social-media channels. The Red Bull Stratos case demonstrates that owned media can itself become valuable enough to attract audiences, rather than functioning merely as a repository for promotional information.

Before-and-After Analysis

Dimension Traditional Advertising Approach Red Bull Stratos Approach

Audience role Advertisement recipient Viewer, participant and sharer

The fundamental transformation can therefore be summarized as: Traditional marketing buys attention. Red Bull Stratos created something capable of earning attention.

Strategic Analysis and Syllabus Linkage

Red Bull Stratos directly demonstrates the relationship between the three digital-media categories covered in Subtopic 1.3:

  • Owned Media — Control: Owned media allowed Red Bull to control the core story, create extensive content and provide destinations where audiences could directly experience the project.
  • Earned Media — Amplification and External Attention: The exceptional nature of the event encouraged journalists, audiences and other external participants to discuss and distribute the story.
  • Paid Media — Reach: Paid and promotional activity could extend awareness and bring additional audiences into the campaign ecosystem.

The strategic model can therefore be represented as: **CREATE** (An extraordinary, brand-relevant experience) → **PUBLISH** (Distribute through owned media) → **PROMOTE** (Use paid amplification to extend awareness) → **EARN** (Generate journalism, sharing and public conversation) → **AMPLIFY** (Earned attention sends more audiences towards the content). The case demonstrates that the categories are most powerful when they are integrated rather than managed as isolated channels.

Key Lessons from the Red Bull Stratos Case

  1. Owned media can become a strategic business asset: Brands no longer have to rely entirely on external publishers to communicate with audiences.
  2. Earned media cannot simply be purchased: Organizations increase the probability of earning attention by creating experiences, information or stories that people genuinely consider worth discussing.
  3. Paid media is most effective when it amplifies something audiences already find valuable: Paid reach can introduce people to content, but the content must provide a reason for continued engagement and sharing.
  4. Brand consistency matters: Red Bull Stratos was highly compatible with Red Bull's existing association with extreme sports, energy and pushing limits. The project therefore strengthened an established brand narrative rather than appearing disconnected from it.

Finally, the case shows that: In digital media, audiences are no longer merely recipients of communication. Through viewing, sharing and discussing content, they can become participants in its distribution.

Conclusion #

Red Bull Stratos is a powerful industrial case study of the integration of owned, earned and paid media.[3] Rather than relying exclusively on conventional advertisements, Red Bull created a real-world event with sufficient human drama, technical ambition and cultural relevance to function as media content in its own right.

Owned media allowed the company to create and distribute extensive branded content and connect directly with audiences. Earned media dramatically expanded the project's visibility as journalists and the public discussed and shared the event. Paid and promotional media formed part of the wider amplification system supporting campaign reach. The mission's digital impact was demonstrated by the more than 8 million concurrent livestreams reported by YouTube at peak.[4]

The most powerful digital-media strategies do not treat owned, earned and paid media as separate channels. They create an integrated system in which each form of media increases the effectiveness of the others.

Red Bull Stratos transformed the company from merely being an advertiser surrounding media content into a brand capable of creating the event, producing the content, attracting the audience and generating worldwide conversation.[5]
